DOCUMENTARY SCRIPT: "A Breath Beyond Earth — The Discovery of LHS 1140b"
Duration: ~10 minutes Format: 2D animated voiceover documentary Tone: Wonder, curiosity, measured optimism

SECTION 1 — THE HOOK (0:00 – 1:15)
[VISUAL: Deep black space. Stars slowly drift. A faint red glow appears in the center. Camera pushes in. Text fades in: "48 light-years away."]

NARRATOR:

Imagine a world so far away that light itself takes forty-eight years to travel from it to us. A world that is not a gas giant, not a frozen wasteland, but a rocky planet — something like Earth — sitting in exactly the right place around its star. Not too hot. Not too cold. Just right.

For decades, we have stared into the dark and asked the same question: Is there another Earth out there? And more importantly — could it have air? Could it breathe?

In the summer of 2026, a team of researchers from Harvard University answered a piece of that question for the very first time. They found something no one had ever confirmed before. Not on a gas planet. Not on a frozen moon. But on a rocky, Earth-like world orbiting inside the habitable zone of another star.

They found an atmosphere.

[VISUAL: An artist's impression of LHS 1140b — a large reddish-brown rocky planet with a faint blue-white atmospheric glow around its edge, a small red star burning in the background.]

This is the story of LHS 1140b — and what its thin shell of helium means for the future of human understanding.

SECTION 2 — WHAT IS THIS TOPIC ABOUT? (1:15 – 2:45)
[VISUAL: Animation of our solar system, then zooming out to show thousands of tiny dots representing discovered exoplanets orbiting distant stars.]

NARRATOR:

Since the first confirmed discovery of an exoplanet — a planet outside our solar system — astronomers have found more than six thousand worlds orbiting distant stars. Some are massive gas giants larger than Jupiter. Others are ice-rich bodies with no solid surface at all. But a small handful are different. They are rocky. They are roughly Earth-sized. And they orbit within what scientists call the habitable zone.

[VISUAL: A diagram of the habitable zone — a green band around a star, labeled "Goldilocks Zone." A planet sits comfortably in the middle.]

The habitable zone, sometimes called the Goldilocks zone, is the region around a star where conditions are neither too extreme nor too mild. It is the orbital distance where liquid water could exist on a planet's surface. And liquid water, as far as we understand biology, is the essential ingredient for life as we know it.

But finding a rocky planet in the habitable zone is only part of the puzzle. A planet could be in the perfect position and still be lifeless — if it has no atmosphere. Without an atmosphere, there is no weather, no protection from radiation, no greenhouse effect to regulate temperature, and no mechanism to cycle gases that living things need.

An atmosphere is the difference between a dead rock and a living world.

And until now, no one had ever confirmed an atmosphere on a rocky exoplanet in the habitable zone of another star.

SECTION 3 — WHAT SCIENTISTS FOUND (2:45 – 4:30)
[VISUAL: The James Webb Space Telescope unfolding in space. Then an animation of light passing through the atmosphere of a distant planet, breaking into a spectrum of colors.]

NARRATOR:

The discovery was led by Dr. Collin Cherubim and Dr. David Charbonneau, both from Harvard University. Their findings were published in the journal Science — one of the most prestigious scientific publications in the world.

The planet they studied is called LHS 1140b. It sits forty-eight light-years from Earth, orbiting a red dwarf star — a star much smaller and cooler than our Sun. Red dwarfs are the most common type of star in the Milky Way, and many of them host rocky planets in their habitable zones.

[VISUAL: An animation showing LHS 1140b orbiting its small red star. The planet passes in front of the star, and a thin beam of starlight filters through its atmosphere.]

To detect the atmosphere, the team used a technique called transmission spectroscopy. As the planet passed in front of its star, a tiny fraction of starlight filtered through the planet's atmosphere. By analyzing the wavelengths of light that were absorbed, the researchers could identify which gases were present.

What they found was helium.

[VISUAL: A spectrograph chart with a clear helium absorption line highlighted. Text appears: "Helium detected."]

Helium, in the upper atmosphere of this planet, was the first confirmed atmospheric detection on a rocky world in the habitable zone of another star. Dr. Cherubim called it "a big deal." And he was right. This was not a tentative signal or a borderline reading. It was the strongest evidence yet that Earth-like planets beyond our solar system can hold onto an atmosphere at all.

SECTION 4 — WHAT MAKES THIS SPECIAL (4:30 – 5:45)
[VISUAL: A side-by-side comparison — Earth with its thick blue atmosphere, and LHS 1140b with a thin glowing haze around its edge. Text reads: "First confirmed atmosphere on a rocky habitable-zone exoplanet."]

NARRATOR:

To understand why this matters, you have to understand how difficult it is to detect an atmosphere on a small rocky planet tens of light-years away. Gas giants like Jupiter have enormous, thick atmospheres that are relatively easy to study. But a rocky planet like Earth — or like LHS 1140b — has a thin, fragile atmospheric shell. Detecting it requires extraordinarily precise measurements of starlight passing through that shell across interstellar distances.

Many scientists had doubts about whether current instruments were sensitive enough to make this detection at all. The fact that they did is a triumph of observational astronomy.

[VISUAL: Animation of other exoplanets — K2-18b, the TRAPPIST-1 system. A question mark appears over each.]

But there is more. LHS 1140b is not the only candidate in the search for life. The sub-Neptune K2-18b made headlines when scientists found possible signs of dimethyl sulfide — a gas linked to marine life on Earth. However, a NASA reanalysis in 2025 showed the signal was too weak to confirm, and that the gas could form without biology. The seven rocky worlds of the TRAPPIST-1 system remain tantalizing, too. NASA's James Webb Space Telescope has already ruled out an Earth-like atmosphere on TRAPPIST-1d, while data on TRAPPIST-1e remain frustratingly inconclusive.

What makes LHS 1140b stand apart is certainty. This is not a maybe. This is a confirmed atmosphere on a rocky planet in the habitable zone. That is a first.

SECTION 5 — WHAT WILL BE THE FUTURE OF THIS (5:45 – 7:00)
[VISUAL: Animation of next-generation space telescopes being designed and launched. A futuristic observatory unfolds in orbit.]

NARRATOR:

The discovery on LHS 1140b is not the end of the search. It is the beginning. Helium alone cannot support life. It is an inert gas — chemically unreactive and biologically useless. But helium in the upper atmosphere strongly suggests that other gases may exist lower down, closer to the surface, where they could interact with water and rock and sunlight.

The next step is to look for those gases. Carbon dioxide. Methane. Water vapor. Oxygen. Any combination of these would transform LHS 1140b from a curiosity into a candidate.

[VISUAL: An animation of the James Webb Space Telescope and the upcoming Habitable Worlds Observatory, both trained on the same point of light in the sky.]

NASA and ESA are already planning the next generation of space telescopes designed specifically for this task. The Habitable Worlds Observatory, expected to launch in the late 2030s, will be capable of directly imaging Earth-like planets and analyzing their atmospheres in far greater detail than Webb can achieve.

Meanwhile, ground-based extremely large telescopes, such as the European Extremely Large Telescope in Chile, will add complementary data from the surface of the Earth.

Within the next two decades, scientists expect to have atmospheric spectra for dozens of rocky habitable-zone planets. LHS 1140b, as the first confirmed case, will remain a primary target.

[VISUAL: A planet with a question mark hovering over it. Slowly, the question mark transforms into a colon and a single word: "Possible."]

The question is no longer whether rocky exoplanets can have atmospheres. The question now is what those atmospheres contain.

SECTION 6 — WHAT THIS MEANS FOR EARTH (7:00 – 8:15)
[VISUAL: Earth from space, slowly rotating. Then a zoom into a forest, a coastline, a city at night. Then a pull-back to show Earth as a tiny dot among billions of stars.]

NARRATOR:

This discovery does not just tell us about a planet forty-eight light-years away. It tells us something about ourselves.

Earth is the only example we have of a living world. Every organism we have ever studied, every ecosystem we have ever mapped, every breath of air we have ever taken exists within a single atmospheric shell around a single rocky planet orbiting a single star. Our entire understanding of what a habitable world looks like is based on a sample size of one.

LHS 1140b changes that. Not because it is identical to Earth — it orbits a red dwarf, it receives different light, its year is different, its temperature profile is different. But because it shows us that atmospheres on rocky worlds are not unique to our solar system. The same physical and chemical processes that built Earth's atmosphere may be at work on worlds orbiting other stars.

[VISUAL: An animation of Earth's early atmosphere forming — volcanoes erupting, gas rising, rain falling, oceans forming.]

If atmospheres can form and persist around rocky planets in the habitable zones of red dwarfs — the most common stars in the galaxy — then the number of potentially habitable worlds just increased by orders of magnitude.

This does not guarantee life. But it removes one of the strongest arguments against it.

SECTION 7 — FURTHER DISCUSSIONS (8:15 – 9:15)
[VISUAL: A panel-style layout — three scientist silhouettes debating. Data visualizations and spectral charts float between them.]

NARRATOR:

Not everyone is ready to celebrate. Some researchers caution that helium alone tells us very little about a planet's potential to support life. Dr. Charbonneau himself was careful to frame the discovery in measured terms. The important thing, he said, was finding an atmosphere. Not proving life.

There are also open questions about how LHS 1140b retained its atmosphere in the first place. Red dwarf stars are notoriously volatile. They emit powerful stellar winds and frequent flares that can strip atmospheres from nearby planets. Some models suggest that rocky planets in the habitable zones of red dwarfs should be barren — stripped down to bare rock by billions of years of stellar radiation.

[VISUAL: An animation showing a red dwarf star flaring violently, its wind blasting a nearby planet. Then a second animation — the same planet with a magnetic field deflecting the wind.]

The fact that LHS 1140b has an atmosphere suggests either that it is more massive than previously thought, or that it has some mechanism — perhaps a magnetic field or a continuous volcanic outgassing — that replenishes what the star takes away.

These are the kinds of questions that will define the next era of exoplanet science. And LHS 1140b, as the first confirmed case, will be at the center of every debate.

SECTION 8 — CONCLUSION (9:15 – 10:00)
[VISUAL: A slow push through a field of stars. The camera approaches LHS 1140b again — the reddish rocky world with its faint blue-white atmospheric glow. The image holds.]

NARRATOR:

Forty-eight light-years away, a small rocky planet orbits a dim red star. It has an atmosphere. It sits in the habitable zone. It is, by every measure we currently have, a world where the conditions for life are not impossible.

We do not know if there is water on its surface. We do not know if there is oxygen in its air. We do not know if anything alive has ever looked up from its surface and wondered about the stars.

But we know this: it has air. And for the first time in the history of science, we can prove it.

This is not the discovery of life. But it is the discovery that the universe builds worlds where life could begin. And that — that single, quiet fact — may be the most important thing we have ever learned.

[VISUAL: The planet fades. The screen goes black. A single line of text appears: "LHS 1140b — 48 light-years from Earth. The first rocky exoplanet with a confirmed atmosphere."]

[VISUAL: Fade to black. End.]

END OF SCRIPT
